\section{4.1 Why we make mistakes}\label{why-we-make-mistakes}

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{(\arabic{enumi})}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Bounded rationality:} bounded by the limits of our capacity to
  obtain info + handle complex issues 
\item
  \textbf{Emotional and stressful} to compare complex items 
\item
  \textbf{Dual Systems} - Kahneman \(\quad\) (a) \textbf{Intuition:}
  quick, easy, autopilot choices (limbic system - emotion, pain,
  pleasure) \(\quad\) (b) \textbf{Reasoning:} deliberate and effortful
  thought (frontal lobe - higher order mental functions)
\end{enumerate}

    \begin{center}\rule{0.5\linewidth}{\linethickness}\end{center}

\section{4.2 Salience}\label{salience}

\textbf{Salience:} how striking or memorable something is Example: CFB
polls weekly AP rankings - win/loss is more salient than margin of
victory, home or away, etc 

    \begin{center}\rule{0.5\linewidth}{\linethickness}\end{center}

\section{4.3 Framing Effects}\label{framing-effects}

\textbf{Framing effects:} how information is presented

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{(\arabic{enumi})}
\item
  \textbf{False beliefs} \(\quad\) (a) Marketers will promote
  misinformation when it yields higher returns \(\quad\) (b) Consumers
  will be more likely to accept misinformation when it makes them
  happier \(\quad\) (c) These beliefs are spread from consumer to
  consumer
\item
  \textbf{Anchoring:} being influenced by irrelevant information
  \(\quad\) Group 1 - How many dates have you been on this month? How
  happy are you? .66 correlation \(\quad\) Group 2 - How happy are you?
  How many dates have you been on this month? .12 correlation \(\quad\)
  For Group 1 - The happiness question was \textbf{anchored} by the
  dating question
\item
  \textbf{Default effects:} Tendency to choose the default option
  \(\quad\) (a) Helps cope with anxiety \(\quad\) (b) Some view the
  default as the recommendation \(\quad\) (c) May prefer the status quo
  and due to loss aversion \(\quad\) Example: Organ donor program in 7
  European countries \(\qquad\) When default is organ donor = 97.4\%
  enrollment \(\qquad\) When default is NOT organ donor = 18\%
  enrollment 
\end{enumerate}

    \begin{center}\rule{0.5\linewidth}{\linethickness}\end{center}

\section{4.4 Poor decisions we make to make ourselves
happy}\label{poor-decisions-we-make-to-make-ourselves-happy}

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{(\arabic{enumi})}
\item
  \textbf{Overconfidence:} People tend to be overconfident when
  assessing their abilities 
\item
  \textbf{Confirmation bias:} People accept info that supports their
  view, reject info that doesn't 
\item
  \textbf{Cognitive Dissonance:} When actions diverge from beliefs
  \(\quad\) Must change belief, change behavior, or accept false belief
\end{enumerate}

    \begin{center}\rule{0.5\linewidth}{\linethickness}\end{center}

\section{4.5 Choice under risk and
uncertainty}\label{choice-under-risk-and-uncertainty}

\textbf{Reference point:} Under uncertainty, people make choices
relative to a reference point (often the status quo or current choice)

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{(\arabic{enumi})}
\item
  \textbf{Reference dependence:} different reference points leads to
  different choices \(\quad\) A rich person would be willing to pay 100
  dollars for high end wine, poor person only willing to pay 5 dollars
\item
  \textbf{Endowment effects:} people place greater value on what they
  already own \(\quad\) Status quo: People prone to making decisions
  consistent with the current state \(\quad\) Loss aversion: eople weigh
  losses heavier than gains
\item
  \textbf{Signals of quality:} people experience greater pleasure from
  product if they think its high quality beforehand
\end{enumerate}

    \begin{center}\rule{0.5\linewidth}{\linethickness}\end{center}

\section{4.6 Time-Inconsistent
Preferences}\label{time-inconsistent-preferences}

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{(\arabic{enumi})}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Discounting:} People tend to value benefits more the sooner
  they are obtained
\end{enumerate}

\(PV(U,t) = D^{t}U\) Present value (PV) of utility (U) recieved in
period (t) = utility (U) times the discount rate (D) raised to the
number of periods ahead (t) Notice that the discount function is
exponential

\textbf{Time inconsistency:} people tend to be more impatient when
comparing today vs tomorrow than when comparing 30 days from now vs 31
days from now
